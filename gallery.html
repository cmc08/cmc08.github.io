I went on a weekend trip with friends recently to Ouray, Colorado—about 5 hours down south from Boulder. We went to go climb in a man-made “ice park”, which would mark my first time trying ice climbing. I knew this would be a great opportunity to take photos for this assignment, so I rented a camera to bring. The Ouray Ice Park is a man-made area, but it looks like part of nature—flowing frozen waterfalls, a large valley full of ice. I wanted to document the intersection between humans and nature.

What I found really intriguing about the environment of the Ouray Ice Park was that you can’t necessarily tell where nature ends and human work begins. It’s this delicate balance between the two—of course not all the ice and snow is man-made, but it’s maintained by workers and sprinklers go off every night to create more ice and maintain the existing ice. I wanted to include pictures of more obvious evidence that humankind has touched this place—glimpses of structures contrasted with the nature around them, but also pictures of just the icy wonderland environment. I found these to be really compelling as they present evidence of humans, but that evidence isn’t apparent or obvious at first glance. In fact, it might not be evident at all without the context. Ice structures can form in nature as well, and it’s hard to spot that it’s man-made without the additional context of the infrastructure around Ouray.

In general I think Ouray is a really interesting place to photograph as the contrast between humans and nature is very apparent there and makes the evidence most obvious.
